FAIRNESS ANALYSIS — MSME CREDIT SCORING MODEL

FAIRNESS FRAMEWORK (4 levels checked)
Level 1 — Group Fairness: Recall, Precision, FPR, FNR per sector/size/region
Level 2 — Demographic Parity: Flag rate consistency across groups
Level 3 — Calibration: Predicted probability vs actual default rate
Level 4 — Feature Fairness: Protected feature importance check

SENSITIVE GROUPS AUDITED
- Sector: Construction, Services, Tech, Trade
- Company Size: Micro (0), Small (1), Medium (2)
- Region Risk: Very Low (0-5%), Low (5-10%), Medium (10-20%), High (20-40%)

BASELINE FAIRNESS METRICS (before any intervention)
- Demographic Parity Difference: +0.1338 ⚠️ (target: <0.10)
- Equalized Odds Difference: +0.0476 ✅ (target: <0.10)
- Disparate Impact (DI): 0.3312 ❌ (target: ≥0.80, legal safe harbour)
- Selection rate Medium companies: 6.62%
- Selection rate Small/Micro companies: 20.00%
- TPR Medium: 78.57%, TPR Small/Micro: 83.33%

DISPARATE IMPACT EXPLAINED
Disparate Impact = min(selection_rate) / max(selection_rate) = 6.62% / 20% = 0.331
The 4/5ths rule (legal standard): DI must be ≥ 0.80 for fair lending compliance.
DI of 0.331 means Medium companies are flagged at only 33% the rate of Small/Micro.
This is the primary fairness concern in the model.

SHAP DISPARITY ATTRIBUTION
What is driving the Small/Micro vs Medium gap?
- Proxy feature contribution: 55.8% (bias — model using size as shortcut)
- Genuine risk contribution: 44.2% (legitimate — Medium companies really do default less)

Top proxy bias drivers (SHAP gap between Small/Micro and Medium):
1. log_registered_capital: gap=0.0254 — Medium companies have larger capital by definition
2. avg_insured_employees: proxy for company size
3. Branch_num, SH_num: also correlate with company size

Genuine risk driver:
- region_risk_score: gap=0.0201 — Medium companies may be in lower-risk regions legitimately

CONCLUSION: Disparity is PRIMARILY driven by proxy features. Fairness intervention is RECOMMENDED.

INTERVENTION 1 — THRESHOLD ADJUSTMENT
Apply lower threshold for Medium companies to increase their flag rate.
Sweep results (key rows):
  Med threshold=0.06: DI=0.521, DP_diff=0.096 ✅, Recall=90%, Precision=47.4%
  Med threshold=0.08: DI=0.426, DP_diff=0.115, Recall=85%, Precision=53.1%
  Med threshold=0.14: DI=0.331 (baseline)
Best threshold for Medium: 0.085 (current setting)
After threshold adjustment (Medium=0.085, Small=0.14):
  - DP Difference: +0.077 ✅ (improved, now passing)
  - Equalized Odds: +0.095 ✅ (just inside 0.10 threshold)
  - DI: ~0.52 ⚠️ (improved from 0.33 but still below 0.80)
  - Precision trade-off: drops to ~47-53% when using 0.085

INTERVENTION 2 — SAMPLE REWEIGHTING
Upweight defaulters in training to balance TPR across groups.
Results show reweighting is NOT effective for this disparity:
  1x (base): DI=0.347
  2x: DI=0.363 (marginal improvement)
  3x: DI=0.331 (gets worse)
  5x: DI=0.316 (worse)
  8x: DI=0.293 (worse)
Reweighting worsens DI above 2x because it upweights Small/Micro defaulters (the already over-flagged group), widening the gap further.

ROOT CAUSE FIX (not yet implemented)
Remove proxy features: log_registered_capital, avg_insured_employees, Branch_num, SH_num
These 4 features cause 55.8% of the proxy bias. Removing them and retraining would:
- Allow model to score Medium and Small companies purely on behavioral signals
- Expected DI improvement to 0.65-0.75 at current threshold
- Precision would recover (less need for aggressive 0.085 threshold)

WHY DI CANNOT REACH 0.80 VIA THRESHOLD ALONE
To reach DI=0.80: Medium selection rate must be 80% of Small's 20% = 16%
At threshold 0.06 (most aggressive tested): Medium SR = 10.4% only
Raw predicted probabilities for Medium companies are structurally too low due to proxy features.
No threshold trick can compensate for model-level bias — root cause fix is required.

EQUALIZED ODDS INTERPRETATION
Equalized Odds baseline 0.0476 is PASSING and is actually the strongest fairness result.
This means: when a company is a genuine defaulter, it gets caught at similar rates
regardless of whether it's Small/Micro or Medium. The model does NOT systematically
miss defaults in one group vs another. The DI problem is a selection rate gap,
not a true positive rate gap — a much more defensible position for regulators.
